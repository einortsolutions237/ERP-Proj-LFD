# LFD Services — Enterprise ERP

## What
Enterprise ERP for LFD Services (starting as a single location, architected for multi-branch growth). A business/retail-and-services enterprise design spanning POS, inventory, CRM, accounting, and HR — LFD Services sells both physical products and services, paid by cash, MTN Mobile Money, or Orange Money.

Stack: Next.js (App Router) + TypeScript + Tailwind CSS, Firebase Auth + Firestore (Native mode, Enterprise edition, africa-south1) + Cloud Functions v2, Blaze tier. Firebase project: `erp-lfd` (not `lfd-erp-4713b`, retired pre-migration). **Deployment target is Vercel per this file's original architecture notes, but no phase across 35 has confirmed a live deployment actually exists — see Phase 35's finding below. Every "live verification" claim in this file, across every phase, was against a local `next dev` server connected to the real `erp-lfd` Firestore — real data, never a real deployed instance.**

Architecture: modular monolith, not microservices — clean module boundaries inside one Next.js app. Every record carries a `branchId` and every permission check is branch-scoped, starting from Phase 1. This was designed against a single branch through Phase 7 and first exercised with a second one in Phase 8, which found the same bug in three places, not two: staff and department *creation*, and — caught mid-phase, easy to miss in a quick audit — department *edit/delete* (`PATCH`/`DELETE /api/departments/[id]`) had it too. All three fixed the same way, via a shared `isBranchLocked(role)` helper (`branch_manager`/`cashier` are branch-locked; everyone else isn't): a branch-locked role's own `branchId` is used unconditionally, an explicit `branchId` in the request body is only honored for non-branch-locked roles, and it's validated against a real `branches` doc before being accepted. Suppliers, customers, products, services, and branches were all checked and correctly confirmed org-wide, needing no such fix — every route that touches `branchId` on read or write now has a documented conclusion, not just the ones that needed changing. Any new route creating a branch-scoped record should use `isBranchLocked` rather than re-deriving the role list.

**Open product question, not a bug** — `GET /api/sales` filters by `user.branchId` unconditionally for every role, including `admin`/`super_admin`, who don't get that restriction anywhere else (staff, departments, stock are all org-wide for them). Whether a location's sales list should stay local even to org-wide roles, or become unfiltered to match the rest of the app, hasn't been decided. `api/sales/route.ts` is protected — don't touch this without an actual decision, and don't assume either answer.

**Fixed and audited (Phase 12)**: `staff/page.tsx`, `departments/page.tsx`, `stock/page.tsx`, and a fourth instance the systematic sweep found, `roles/page.tsx`, all now use `isBranchLocked()` matching their sibling routes exactly, instead of the hardcoded branch filter that used to bypass Phase 8's fix entirely. Fixing stock's read also surfaced and fixed a related latent bug: `StockTable.tsx` was passing the *viewer's own* ambient `branchId` into every row's Adjust/Transfer form regardless of that row's actual branch — harmless while the read bug hid multi-branch rows, live-reachable the moment it didn't. Each row now carries and uses its own `branchId` throughout, including in the transfer destination-exclusion logic, which was a genuine correctness improvement, not just a rewrite (an org-wide admin viewing a foreign branch's row can now correctly transfer stock into their own branch, previously wrongly excluded). Minor, pre-existing, unrelated finding surfaced along the way: `stock/page.tsx`'s low-stock indicator uses `quantity < reorderThreshold` while the shared `isLowStock()` (used by reports and the Cloud Function) uses `<=` — boundary-only, fix whenever something else touches that file by switching to the shared helper instead of its own inline check. `pos/sales/page.tsx` mirrors `GET /api/sales`'s own unconditional branch filter and was correctly left alone — that route was never given the Phase 8 fix in the first place, it's the still-open product question below, not a bug with a known pattern to restore.

**LFD Services is a hybrid business, confirmed** — the retail/services operation already built, plus a genuine clinical operation, same people: a retail customer can also become a patient. This is new as of the clinical-module conversation, not something the original 12 phases were built against. Core design decision: patients are not a new entity — they're the existing `customers` collection, with clinical data (treatment/visit records, lab results, seminar attendance) living in separate, strictly access-walled collections referenced by `customerId`, not fields on the customer doc itself. A person becomes a "patient" the moment a clinical record exists for them; nothing else about their existing commercial record changes. Clinical data is visible to `doctor`, `medical_secretary` (Phase 13.1, full read like doctor, no authoring), `general_manager` (full read like doctor/medical_secretary, no authoring — explicit override, general_manager was originally proposed as excluded, corrected by direct instruction), and `super_admin` only — not `admin` despite being broad elsewhere, not `branch_manager`, `cashier`, `hr_admin`, `finance_admin`, or `it_admin`. Every read is still audit-logged regardless of who's reading — broadening who holds the capability doesn't broaden it without a trail. `medical_secretary` also gets `crm.customer.view` (payment/purchase history) — the first role that legitimately spans both the commercial and clinical sides of the wall, since that's what the job actually is, not a breach of it. Treatment/consultation payment reuses the existing POS flow entirely — a consultation is a `service` in the existing catalog, sold through the already-audited checkout — so payment history needs no new infrastructure, only the clinical record itself, appointments, lab, and seminars are genuinely new. Appointments are new and specific to clinical visits — they coexist with, not replace, the confirmed walk-in model for retail/spa-style services; a doctor visit is the one appointment-based exception, not a reversal of the Phase 2 decision. Roadmap: Phase 13 (clinical foundation — audited, closed pending manual deletion only), Phase 13.1 (`medical_secretary` role — audited, zero findings), Phase 14 (appointments, in progress — staff books on the patient's behalf, confirmed; no patient self-service, that would be a genuinely different kind of system, not an extension of this one), Phase 15 (laboratory — test orders and results), Phase 16 (health seminars — events, `protocol` records attendance, `medical_secretary` gets the oversight view).

**Appointments (Phase 14) join the clinical wall, not a separate visibility tier** — who's seeing which doctor when is itself patient-identifying health information, so appointment access uses the same group as clinical records: `doctor`, `medical_secretary`, `general_manager`, `admin`, `super_admin` — not `branch_manager`, populating the `clinical.appointments.manage` capability reserved back in Phase 13.1. Viewing the schedule is read-audit-logged the same way treatment views are, for the same reason. Scope deliberately limited: conflict prevention (no double-booking the same doctor) is in, a doctor working-hours/availability configuration system is not — any date/time can be proposed, only overlaps get rejected. Linking a resulting treatment record back to the appointment that led to it is deferred, not forgotten — touching `api/treatments/route.ts` again isn't worth the added scope for this phase specifically. Audited, zero findings in the feature itself. `clinical.appointments.manage` is granted via the exact same constant reference as `clinical.record.view` (`CLINICAL_VIEW_ROLES`), not a separate list — the two capabilities structurally cannot drift apart.

**Corrected during Phase 14: `admin` had been in the clinical-access role lists since Phase 13, contradicting this file's own stated design, uncaught through two of my own audits (13 and 13.1).** Now fixed — `CLINICAL_ROLES` (`clinical.record.create`) is `['super_admin', 'doctor']`, `CLINICAL_VIEW_ROLES` (`clinical.record.view`, `clinical.appointments.manage`) is `['super_admin', 'doctor', 'medical_secretary']` — no `admin` in either, independently confirmed by reading the constants directly. Root cause: this file's design changed (narrowing clinical access away from `admin`) without ever generating an actual task to update the code, and the gap wasn't caught until a phase that happened to diff the code against this file's stated design rather than just checking new work for internal consistency. Lesson for future audits of this project: when this file's design changes, verify the code actually caught up, don't just verify the newest phase is self-consistent.

**Roles restructuring (Phase 17), audited, zero findings.** `admin`'s final capability set is exactly five items, independently confirmed by reading raw source: `admin.staff.create`/`admin.roles.view`/`admin.roles.assign` (via `ADMIN_HR`), `admin.settings.manage`/`admin.auditLog.view` (via `ADMIN_IT`) — nothing else, `admin` appears nowhere else in `permissions.ts`. `general_manager` confirmed present in `STRICT_AUDIT_ROLES`, `CLINICAL_VIEW_ROLES` (by reference), `SEMINAR_MANAGE_ROLES`, `SEMINAR_VIEW_ROLES`, `CATALOG_MANAGE_ROLES`, `REPORTS_ROLES`, `APPROVER_ROLES`, `GENERAL_MANAGER_BRANCH_MGR`, `GENERAL_MANAGER_HR`, `GENERAL_MANAGER_ONLY`. **Messaging, proposed, not yet built** — two genuinely different channels, not one module. A hierarchical line: staff reach only their own branch's `branch_manager`; `branch_manager` reaches their own branch's staff plus `general_manager`; `general_manager` reaches only `branch_manager`s, not staff directly. This is a *relationship* check (can this specific sender reach that specific recipient given their branch and role), not a capability check like everything else in this system — genuinely new category of access logic, not an extension. Separately, a flat IT-support line: anyone ↔ `it_admin`, no hierarchy. Delivery should likely reuse Phase 6's Cloud-Function-driven notification pattern rather than parallel infrastructure — a new message triggering a `notifications` doc the same way a low-stock crossing or leave submission already does. Treat as its own phase(s), after the clinical roadmap, not folded into it.



## Why
LFD Services needs one system to replace fragmented/manual operations — starting with staffing and access control, extending to POS, inventory, CRM, accounting, and HR. Full module vision: `docs/project-brief.md`.

## How

**Permissions** — 7 roles: super_admin (protected, immutable, cannot be demoted or deleted in-app), admin, branch_manager, hr_admin, finance_admin, it_admin, cashier (added Phase 3 — frontline POS role, no admin/inventory/supplier access anywhere). Permissions are mapped per module (admin, POS, inventory, CRM, accounting, HR) — not by role alone — and enforced at three layers: UI, server, Firestore rules, never just one. Firebase custom claims (`role`, `branchId`) are the sole source of truth for authorization; Firestore is profile metadata only and is never read to make an access-control decision. `branch_manager` has full catalog-adjacent access (suppliers, stock) but not `inventory.catalog.manage` itself — product/service definitions are admin/super_admin only, a deliberate split from Phase 2, not an oversight.

**Never repeat these — each caused a real production issue in an earlier build of this project:**
- No Proxy-based Firebase SDK initialization — use direct SDK calls
- No hardcoded Firebase credentials or fallback values in client code — env vars only, no `??` fallback to literals
- Don't let tooling silently regenerate/empty `tsconfig.json` — verify it after any AI-assisted scaffold
- No `Math.random()` for temporary passwords or any security-sensitive value — use a CSPRNG
- Firestore rules default-deny; every collection needs an explicit, tested rule — no blanket permissive rules
- Session cookies: don't hardcode `secure: true` + `sameSite: none` — it silently drops cookies in non-HTTPS preview environments
- No public self-registration route — accounts are created by a permitted internal role only
- Cloud Functions v2, not v1 (deprecated)
- No unauthenticated, unthrottled endpoint that checks a password or secret — rate-limit it (Phase 1 audit finding, fixed in Phase 2: see `src/lib/auth/rate-limit.ts`, Firestore-backed, transaction-safe, per-email and per-IP)
- Any read-check-write sequence on a numeric quantity needs a real Firestore transaction, not a plain read followed by a separate write (stock movements/transfers do this correctly; this applies to POS stock decrements too — reuse that exact pattern, don't write a parallel one)

**Known-issues policy** — check `docs/tech-debt.md` and this file before starting a phase. If the phase's own work already touches the same file or area as something already flagged, fix it as part of that work and say so in the plan and the completion report. If it doesn't touch that area, just re-flag it rather than going out of scope to chase it down — a phase stays bounded to what it was asked to build, it doesn't turn into a standing bug hunt. Currently open: TD-1 (audit-log entries for `product_edit`/`service_edit`/`supplier_edit`/`customer_create` don't capture full before/after or creation detail), TD-2 (`onLowStock`'s reconstructed "quantity before this movement" has a race window if a second movement for the same product+branch lands before the trigger reads it — fixable only by adding a `resultingQuantity` snapshot to the stock-movement write path itself, which needs the same review rigor as any other change to `api/stock/movements/route.ts`/`api/stock/transfer/route.ts`/`api/sales/route.ts`), and TD-3 — **closed permanently, not deferred**: the allowlist-per-collection approach stays. Six collections checked independently as of Phase 18 (`sales`, `treatments`, `appointments`, `labOrders`, `seminarAttendance`, `pendingDeliveries`), with the honest `pendingDeliveries` nuance on record — it can never independently trigger the block, since a pending delivery only ever exists alongside a `sales` doc for the same customer, so the `sales` check (which runs first) is always what actually blocks deletion for this case. Still correct defense-in-depth regardless. No soft-delete/archive migration is planned — any future dependent collection gets the same one-line check added, permanently, as the standing pattern.

**Backorder & pending delivery (Phase 18), audited, zero Critical/Important findings — the first deliberate modification to `api/sales/route.ts`'s core transaction since Phase 3.** Read directly and confirmed: `quantityTaken = Math.min(currentQuantity, pl.quantity)` means a normal, sufficiently-stocked sale computes byte-identical values to the pre-Phase-18 logic — the regression case holds structurally, not just by a passing test. `POS_DELIVERY_FULFILL_ROLES = [super_admin, general_manager, branch_manager, cashier]`, confirmed by reading the constant — no `admin`, exactly the capability that would have needed retrofitting had this shipped before Phase 17. Full-quantity fulfillment only; partial fulfillment and the actual offline queue are Phase 18.1.

**Offline queue & sync (Phase 18.1), in progress.** IndexedDB for the local queue, not localStorage. A queued sale gets a client-generated, never-regenerated idempotency key at creation. `POST /api/sales` gains a second deliberate touch this phase — an idempotency-key check inside the same transaction, returning the existing sale if the key was already processed, rather than creating a duplicate — the mechanism that actually prevents a double-sale when a sync response is lost after the server already committed. A sync failure that's a real business rejection (the backorder-without-customer case from Phase 18 is the one that matters most, since an offline device can't know in advance whether a shortfall will occur) becomes `needs_attention`, distinct from `queued` (still waiting on connectivity) — the queue doesn't block behind it. Scope is one device, one tab — no multi-device conflict resolution, no background sync.

One real gap caught mid-phase and fixed before shipping, worth remembering as a gap in the original phase prompt, not the execution: `customers/[id]/page.tsx`'s top-level reachability guard needed `seminars.attendance.view` added to it, or `protocol` could hold every capability it needed and still never reach the page — fixed, confirmed live both directions (`protocol` sees only Seminar attendance, `cashier` sees only Purchase History on the same page). **Laboratory (Phase 15), audited, zero findings.** `clinical.lab.manage` (`CLINICAL_ROLES` by reference — `super_admin`, `doctor`, deliberately not `medical_secretary`, since results entry stays with the same actor set as authoring treatments) and `clinical.lab.view` (`CLINICAL_VIEW_ROLES` by reference) both confirmed correct. Results are structured value/unit/reference-range/flag lines, not free text. Result entry is transaction-guarded — atomically writes the result and flips the order to `completed`, rejecting re-entry against an already-completed order. The Phase 13 lab placeholder on the customer detail page and in `ClinicalSection.tsx` is now replaced with real data; the Phase 16 seminar-attendance placeholder in the same file is confirmed untouched.

**Notifications are Cloud-Function-driven, not bolted into existing routes** — confirmed working as intended in Phase 6: the four already-audited routes it could have touched instead came out unmodified, verified directly. A Firestore trigger listens for writes that already happen (`stockMovements`, `leaveRequests`) and creates a `notifications` doc reactively. Every notification write uses `create()` against a deterministic doc ID, not `set()` — Cloud Functions redeliver events at-least-once, and `create()` makes a retry fail loudly (caught and swallowed) instead of silently double-writing.

**Reporting reads on-demand, no new collections** — a report is computed at request time from `sales`/`stockMovements`/`productStock`, not maintained as a running aggregate the way `productStock.quantity` is. Fine at current data volume; revisit toward pre-aggregated summary docs only if a report route becomes slow as history grows — don't build that machinery preemptively. Revenue figures always exclude voided sales (`voidedAt != null`) from the totals, but a report should still surface how many sales were voided and their value alongside the real total, not hide them entirely.

**Audit log** — every login, failed login, logout, and create/edit/delete on customers, staff, catalog/stock, sales, or permissions gets an immutable log entry, written server-side only. Never expose an edit or delete path for audit entries. Known gap (tracked in `docs/tech-debt.md` as TD-1): catalog edit actions (`product_edit`/`service_edit`/`supplier_edit`) record that a change happened but not what changed. Stock, sales, and permission-change actions carry full detail in `details` — sale creation should follow that same fuller pattern (line items, total, payment breakdown), not the bare catalog-edit one.

**Login has two paths, chosen server-side by account role, not by the client:**
- `super_admin` and `admin` (the roles where a failed-login attempt is the highest-value security signal) verify the password server-side against the Identity Toolkit REST API. Every attempt — success or failure — is written to the audit log by code we control; this path is tamper-proof.
- `branch_manager`, `hr_admin`, `finance_admin`, `it_admin`, and `cashier` sign in via the Firebase client SDK directly. A failed attempt never reaches our server on its own — the client reports it best-effort after the fact. Treat these `login_failed` entries as telemetry, not a tamper-proof control; this is an accepted trade-off, not a gap to silently fix later. `cashier` belongs on this path deliberately — high-frequency, low-privilege logins are exactly what it's for.
- The routing decision (which path a given email uses) is made by a pre-flight server lookup of the account's role before any password is checked — this necessarily reveals whether a given email belongs to a `super_admin`/`admin` account (a narrow account-role signal, not full account-existence enumeration — unknown emails and non-strict-role emails get an identical response). Accepted for an internal-only ERP; revisit if this app ever gets external-facing accounts. This signal is also visible via response timing, not just response shape, since the strict path makes an extra network call before responding — same accepted trade-off, second channel.

**Catalog & stock ownership** — `products` and `services` are org-wide catalog entries, not branch-scoped (a SKU/price is the same everywhere). What's branch-specific is quantity on hand: `productStock` holds one doc per branch+product, and its `quantity` is a derived, server-maintained total — never directly settable. Every stock change — restock/adjustment/waste/transfer/**sale** — writes an immutable `stockMovements` ledger entry, and the same Admin-SDK write path atomically increments `productStock.quantity` in the same transaction. No edit form ever sets quantity directly.

**Services are walk-in, not appointment-based** — confirmed during Phase 2: no scheduling, calendar, or slot-picker concept anywhere. A service is sold and rendered in one motion, same as a product, just without a stock count behind it.

**Payments** — cash, MTN Mobile Money, and Orange Money, confirmed for Phase 3. A sale can split across any combination of the three. This is manual recording only, by design — no payment-gateway or mobile-money API integration of any kind, no webhook, no balance/transaction verification against either provider. The cashier checks payment on their own phone, outside the app entirely, and just tells the system which method was used; the app's only job is to record that claim. Mobile-money lines (either provider) carry an optional reference/confirmation code the cashier can type in for later reconciliation; cash lines don't. Tax is a stubbed `taxAmount` field defaulting to 0 until jurisdiction is locked — don't build real tax-rate logic yet.

**Voiding a sale never rewrites it** — a void adds `voidedAt`/`voidedBy`/`voidReason` to the existing sale doc; the original line items, prices, and payments are never altered or removed. Reversal is ledger-driven, not re-derived from `lineItems`: it queries the original sale's actual `stockMovements` entries (by `saleId` + `type: 'sale'`) and negates each one, rather than re-reading the sale's line items and recomputing what should be reversed — undoing exactly what the ledger recorded as having happened, not re-deriving intent from a separate structure. Each reversal writes a new `stockMovements` entry (type `void`, positive delta, same `saleId` link) and atomically increments `productStock` back up, in the same transaction as the sale-doc update. Only `branch_manager` (their own branch only), `admin`, and `super_admin` can void — not `cashier`, deliberately: the same reasoning as the strict login path applies here, the person best positioned to abuse a control shouldn't be the one holding it unsupervised. A sale can be voided exactly once — the transaction checks `voidedAt` is unset before proceeding. Whenever revenue reporting gets built, voided sales need to be excluded from totals; they stay in the collection for the audit trail, not folded into anything financial.

**Customers are org-wide, like products** — a customer is the same person everywhere, not owned by whichever branch first registered them; only individual sales carry which branch a purchase actually happened at. Phone number is the unique identifier — enforce that with a real transaction-wrapped check, the same fix already applied to the SKU race condition in Phase 2, not a plain read-then-write repeating that mistake. Linking a customer to a sale is optional, not required — walk-in/anonymous sales work exactly as they did in Phase 3; CRM sits on top of checkout, it doesn't gate it.

**Nurse & patient intake (Phase 19.1), audited, zero Critical findings.** Three genuinely different pieces of data, not one: `patientDemographics` (marital status, religion, occupation, referral — one-time facts about the person, referenced by `customerId`, updatable but not re-entered per visit — clinical-adjacent, not on the general `customers` record every role can see); `intakeQuestionnaire` (a single shared, nurse-editable template — an ordered list of question texts, not patient data at all); `nursingVisits` (per-visit vitals — height/weight/blood pressure — and questionnaire answers, with the question *text* snapshotted onto each answer at record time, not a live reference to the template, same reasoning as a sale snapshotting a product's name/price rather than pointing at the live catalog). `clinical.intake.record` (write access to both demographics and per-visit vitals/answers) is `nurse`, `doctor`, `super_admin` — the doctor needs to gather or correct this data directly when the nurse isn't available or got something wrong, not just view what's already there, so this isn't the narrow nurse-only specialist capability `clinical.lab.manage` is. `clinical.questionnaire.manage` (editing the shared template) is `nurse`, `admin`, `general_manager`, `super_admin`. `clinical.intake.view` is a genuinely separate role list — `super_admin`, `doctor`, `medical_secretary`, `general_manager`, `nurse` — **not** composed from `CLINICAL_VIEW_ROLES` by reference, since that constant also backs `clinical.record.view` and `clinical.appointments.manage`, and reusing it would silently grant `nurse` the deep clinical/diagnosis access just confirmed as out of scope. The doctor needs `clinical.intake.view` for the actual reason this workflow exists — reviewing the nurse's intake before the consultation — while `nurse` itself does not get `clinical.record.view` in the other direction. `nurse` has no appointment-schedule visibility in this phase — a deliberate, confirmed-lower-stakes scope limit, not an oversight.


**Messaging (Phase 19), audited, zero Critical findings — the first relationship-based access check in this system, not a capability check.** Every prior permission has been "does this role hold this capability," evaluated independently of who else is involved. Messaging needs "can this specific sender reach that specific recipient," which depends on both people's role *and* branch together: a staff member (anyone who isn't `branch_manager`/`general_manager`/`it_admin`) can only reach their own branch's `branch_manager`; a `branch_manager` can reach their own branch's staff plus any `general_manager`; a `general_manager` can reach any `branch_manager`, org-wide, but not staff directly. Separately, a flat line: anyone can reach `it_admin` for support, and `it_admin` can reach anyone back — no hierarchy on that line. `super_admin` can reach and be reached by anyone, consistent with its unrestricted position everywhere else in this system. `admin` and `it_admin` (outside the support line) are treated as generic staff for hierarchy purposes — they reach their own branch's `branch_manager` like everyone else, an assumption worth confirming rather than a settled design call. Conversations use a deterministic ID from the sorted participant-uid pair, same idempotent-creation pattern already used for attendance records — a conversation between two given people can only ever be one document, not create duplicates on repeated lookups. The relationship check runs on every message send, not just at conversation creation, since a role or branch reassignment after a conversation exists should close off a channel that's no longer valid, not leave it open on trust. Delivery reuses Phase 6's Cloud-Function-driven notification pattern. No real-time/websocket layer — consistent with this app's established "read on demand" stance (same reasoning as Phase 7's reporting), refresh/poll-based for a first version. Message creation is logged; reading a conversation is not — this is private staff communication, not patient data, and audit-logging every read would be noise, not a safeguard, unlike the clinical-record case.
